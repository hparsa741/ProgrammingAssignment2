Air Pollution pm2.5 - 1999 vs 2012

In this report we look at the data that comes from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and it involves air pollution monitoring data from the United States. In particular were looking at fine particulate matter air pollution or pm that is in the air. This is a health concern since we inhale the air and along with that, the particulate matter or dust will be inhaled as well. One of the important pieces of legislation in the last four decades in the United States has been the Clean Air Act. The Clean Air Act has been designed to reduce the levels of air pollution in the United States. So the first question that you might ask is, are air pollution levels lower now than they were before? Were going to approach answering this question in a very specific way by looking at a fine particle air pollution that was started being measured in 1999 until today. And also answering the question, Are the levels on average lower in 2012 than they were in 1999? Air pollution data in the United States is collected by EPA and is made freely available on their website based on FOIA.

In the folowing peice of R code we download the files for both 1999 and 2012 years from the website, read and clean the data.

temp <- tempfile()
download.file("http://www.epa.gov/ttn/airs/airsaqs/detaildata/501files/Rd_501_88101_1999.Zip",temp)
pm0 <- read.table(unz(temp, "RD_501_88101_1999-0.txt"), comment.char = "#", header = F, sep = "|", na.string="")
unlink(temp)

temp <- tempfile()
download.file("http://www.epa.gov/ttn/airs/airsaqs/detaildata/501files/RD_501_88101_2012.zip",temp)
pm1 <- read.table(unz(temp, "RD_501_88101_2012-0.txt"), comment.char = "#", header = F, sep = "|", na.string="")
cnames <- readLines(unz(temp, "RD_501_88101_2012-0.txt"), 1)
unlink(temp)

names(pm0) <- make.names(strsplit(cnames, "|", fixed = T)[[1]])
names(pm1) <- names(pm0)

To compare how the data is spread across the ranges well take a look at boxplot of sample values. To fix the right skewness of data however, we will be plotting the log of the values.

We also noticed in the summary of 2012 data that there is negative values. negative values basically cannot be correct because pm is the measure of mass which cannot be negative. We create a logical vetor depending on the measured value being below zero.

negative <- x1<0

For the year 2012 there are 51923 records or 2.31 percent of the entire data set having negative value. Capturing negative values might have happened at certain time of the year. To look into that well extract the dates first.

dates<-pm1$Date
dates<-as.Date(as.character(dates), "%Y%m%d")

It seems negative values are more often in colder months of the year. To have a more consistent view of the data, well take a look at monitoring sites at only one site in one county and in one state, e.g New York with state code of 36, county code of 63 and site code of 2008. Then compare the records of the sites that exist in both years, 1999 and 2012.

Now well show state-wide means and make a plot showing trend

mn0 <- with(pm0, tapply(Sample.Value, State.Code, mean, na.rm = T))
mn1 <- with(pm1, tapply(Sample.Value, State.Code, mean, na.rm = T))
d0 <- data.frame(state = names(mn0), mean = mn0)
d1 <- data.frame(state = names(mn1), mean = mn1)
mrg <- merge(d0, d1, by = "state")
par(mfrow = c(1, 1))
with(mrg, plot(rep(1, 53), mrg[, 2], xlim = c(.5, 2.5)))
